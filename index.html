babies were born to girls aged 14 and younger in Brazil in 2024

By Alexa Meirelles | July 2026

In Brazil, every girl under the age of 14 who becomes pregnant is legally entitled to an abortion. Yet in 2024, 12,007 babies were born to mothers in this age group, an average of nearly 33 births every day.

Even though abortion is a criminal offense in Brazil, their situation fits within one of the three exceptions: the pregnancy results from rape. Under the country's law, any sexual act involving a person under the age of 14 is legally classified as statutory rape, regardless of whether the victim claims there was consent.

Data from SINASC (Sistema de Informações Sobre Nascidos Vivos, en: Brazil Live Birth Information System), of the Ministry of Health, shows that births to girls aged 14 and younger accounted for approximately 5 per 1,000 live births in 2024, a considerable decrease from the past — twenty years earlier, that figure was 8.6, when 26,276 cases were recorded.

A nationwide picture

São Paulo recorded the largest absolute number of births to girls aged 14 and younger in 2024, about 1,216. This is unsurprising given that it is Brazil's most populous state, with roughly 46 million residents according to the 2022 national census.

However, when the figures are adjusted per 1,000 live births, a different pattern emerges. The highest rates are concentrated in Brazil's Northern region, led by Acre (13.3), Amazonas (12.3), Roraima (11.9), Amapá (11.6), and Maranhão (10.2, this last one is in the Northeast region). For comparison, the states with the lowest rates is Santa Catarina (2.1), in the South region.

Who are these girls?

The records also offer a snapshot of the girls who gave birth in 2024. The vast majority were 13 or 14 years old, accounting for 96.3% of the records. The youngest age recorded in the data was eight, with three births recorded among girls that young.

Most were registered as single (87.6%), as expected, but at least one in ten (11.3%) were recorded as married or living in a stable union. In Brazil, people can marry without parental consent from the age of 18, when they reach legal adulthood, or from the age of 16 with the authorization of their parents or legal guardians. Marital status was not recorded in 0.4% of the cases.

About 68.1% were registered as Parda, while 6.3% were registered as Black. Together, these groups accounted for 74.4% of the records. White girls represented 16.9%, Indigenous girls 6.5%, and 0.3% were identified as Asian. Race/color was not recorded in 2% of cases.

The legal right to abortion at risk

In early June, Brazil's Federal Senate approved a Legislative Decree Bill (PDL) suspending Conanda's (Conselho Nacional dos Direitos da Criança e do Adolescente, en: Brazil's National Council for the Rights of Children and Adolescents) Resolution No. 258, issued in 2024. The resolution established national guidelines for assisting children and adolescents who become pregnant as a result of rape, including access to legal abortion.

The bill, introduced by federal congresswoman Chris Tonietto (PL–Rio de Janeiro), had already been approved by the Chamber of Deputies in November 2025. Its supporters argued that Conanda had exceeded its authority by regulating matters that should be decided by the country's Congress.

The resolution instructed healthcare and child protection professionals on how to assist victims of sexual violence while reducing the risk of revictimization. It also reaffirmed that access to legal abortion in cases of rape does not require a police report or prior judicial authorization.

Suspending the resolution does not change Brazil's abortion law, but child rights organizations argue that removing these guidelines could make it harder for victims to receive timely care and protection.

After the bill was approved by the Chamber of Deputies, the Ministry of Women issued a statement warning that forced pregnancy is the leading cause of girls dropping out of school and results in the death of one girl every week in Brazil.

“Conanda's resolution, developed with the participation of civil society, neither exceeds the council's authority nor creates new rights. It simply explains how existing law should be applied to save lives. Suspending this measure means turning a blind eye to violence and failing Brazilian girls.”
